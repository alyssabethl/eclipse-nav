Introduction

What Are Baily's Beads Anyway?

During a total solar eclipse, when the Moon completely covers the Sun, some sunlight escapes from the edges in an irregular pattern. This phenomenon occurs due to the rugged surface of the Moon, where valleys and craters allow small bursts of sunlight to pass through, forming what is known as Baily’s beads. This effect was first vividly described by English astronomer, Francis Baily, in 1836, following his observations of an annular solar eclipse. His detailed account led to the epony- mous naming of the phenomenon.



An important aspect of Baily's Beads is that they do not shine with equal intensity from all craters, lunar mountains, and lunar valleys. We also know that not all lunar topography is visible from everywhere on Earth. With that said, it is inevitable to deduce that the appearance and brightness of Baily’s beads vary depending on the observer’s position.These facts can be utilized to determine the observer’s precise position. By leveraging lunar limb profiles derived from topographic datasets, such as those obtained from the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO), we reconstruct the expected bead patterns for various geographic locations. We then apply image processing and feature extraction techniques to eclipse im- ages to match the observed bead sequences with predicted models, thus estimating the photographer’s position.


This project is the development of a computational pipeline that combines image-based detection of Baily’s Beads, 3D lunar topography (SLDEM2015[8]), and ephemeris-based observer modeling. By extracting limb brightness profiles from eclipse images and matching them with simulated limb projections based on known crater geometry, we identify the specific lunar features responsible for bead formation. Using this crater-bead correspondence, we then reverse engineer the eclipse geometry to estimate the observer’s terrestrial coordinates.


This methodology introduces a novel approach to GPS-free Earth localization using natural astronomical events, with potential applications in celestial navigation, eclipse path validation, and lunar surface modeling. The results demonstrate that Baily’s Beads are not merely beautiful but can serve as measurable, reproducible, and location-dependent signals in computational perception.

Related Work


  • In 2020, researchers at Wuhan University developed an algorithm to compute the geometric component of sky irradiation during an eclipse using Moon topography from the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO) mission. They also modeled the Baily’s beads of the 2012 solar eclipse observed in Lakeland, Australia (source). This inspired the use of Lunar Orbiter Laser Altimeter (LOLA) images for accurate coordinate calculations.
  • A 2021 paper titled “Lunar Crater Identification in Digital Images” addressed the challenge of identifying lunar craters in single images without known camera location. It introduced pre-computed mathematical descriptors for recognizing crater patterns (source).
  • Jayatissa (2021) studied the detection of disappearing Baily’s beads during a solar eclipse using intensity tracking in video footage. The study pinpointed video frames marking the end of the beads, using Limovie software to analyze footage from the December 26, 2019, annular eclipse (source).
  • One key contribution was the manual detection of 37 disappearing beads tracked frame-by-frame to create light intensity curves. Challenges such as codec incompatibility and file size limits were overcome using preprocessing tools like VirtualDub. This dataset supports future solar radius estimation and lunar limb reconstruction.
  • Although limited by software and lacking automation or geolocation, the study demonstrated the potential of eclipse-based celestial measurements. It also highlighted opportunities for future work using high-resolution data and modern algorithms.
  • A 1975 paper gave historical insight into Baily’s bead observations during the June 1974 eclipse. It linked bead sequences to specific lunar topographical features, improving understanding of the Moon’s limb profile and eclipse prediction accuracy (source).
